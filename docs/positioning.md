# The Workshop — positioning (Satya + Yegge)

Why The Workshop is the thing the industry is converging on, from two directions: Microsoft's CEO
(Satya Nadella) and the practitioner zeitgeist (Steve Yegge's "Flat Curve Society" / AI literacy /
the discernment horizon). Captured 2026-06-22.

## The one-liner
**The Workshop is how humans stay in control of -- and get frontier-quality work from -- the
*accessible* models.** That's the central problem both Satya and Yegge just named, and The
Workshop is a concrete answer to it.

## The convergence

### Tie 1 (deepest): the discernment horizon -- The Workshop is supervisability, productized
Yegge's load-bearing point: past some capability you can't *verify* the output ("superhuman means
unverifiable"). That's why the frontier gets locked down *and* why a rational buyer prefers the
safer, checkable model. The binding constraint of the era is not intelligence; it's
**supervisability**.

The Workshop raises the human's discernment horizon. A room of accessible, plateau-class desks
that (a) show their reasoning, (b) are free to disagree, and (c) check each other against external
facts produces output you *can* verify -- where one black-box super-answer you could not. The
concrete primitive for (c) is the **isolated fresh-context reviewer with an execution receipt**: a
desk that didn't author the work runs the build, checks the contract, and emits PASS / PASS-WITH /
SEND-BACK. That's Yegge's "grown-up human supervision," built and demoable.
And his "discernment-horizon-as-infrastructure" (route each task to the dumbest model that can
handle it; escalate the hard ones) is exactly a **trust ladder**: cheap desks for the bulk,
escalate on low confidence, hands-up at the edge.

### Tie 2: a hands-on lab *is* the multi-agent literacy course
Yegge / Netflix (the Ezra Savard study) defines AI-literacy cohorts by token spend: 0 ->
single-agent (4M/day) -> **multi-agent, 2-4 agents unwatched (12-15M/day)**. The top cohort *is*
The Workshop. And the training that *sticks* (Netflix's validated formula): team-at-a-time,
manager opted in, blessed company time, **bring real work, hands-on**. That is the lab -- not
"demo a tool," but teaching the key literacy of 2026-27.
**Honest catch:** the format only *sticks* at 5-10 people per team. Hundreds at once buys the aha,
not the durable skill. Run a big lab as the **spark at scale**, with small-team cohorts behind it
as the part that sticks.

### Tie 3: The Workshop is Satya's "continuous learning system," on the ground
Satya's AI-first org = a continuous learning system combining human expertise + AI-generated
knowledge, drawing on multiple AI systems of varying capability/cost, while keeping control of its
own data and IP, and capturing institutional knowledge as the durable source of advantage. The
Workshop is the on-the-ground form: the operator (human expertise) + the desks (AI knowledge, any
model, any cost), running *in your repo* (your IP -- nobody ships their code to a model vendor),
with the desks' memory / journals / fable = the institutional knowledge captured as a durable
asset.

## Why it gets *more* valuable as models plateau
Yegge: the craft "only gets harder, and more valuable, the weaker your models are." A well-run room
is how you get frontier-quality results from plateau-class, broadly-available models -- so The
Workshop's value rises exactly as the accessible frontier flattens.

## Tensions to hold honestly
- **Hundreds vs. small-team stickiness** (Tie 2). Spark at scale + cohorts behind it.
- **The discernment ceiling.** A room *raises* the discernment horizon; it doesn't abolish it. For
  genuinely superhuman / unverifiable work, even a room only pushes the line out. (Some classes of
  work stay human-in-the-loop forever.)
- **Token cost.** Hundreds running rooms is real spend (the multi-agent cohort burns 12-15M
  tok/day). But Yegge reframes efficiency as *the* advanced literacy ("great outcomes with the
  least spend"), so token-efficiency becomes something the lab *teaches*, not just a constraint to
  fight.
